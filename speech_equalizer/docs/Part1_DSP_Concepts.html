1.1 What is Sound, Physically?

Sound is vibration. When someone speaks, their vocal cords vibrate, pushing air molecules back and forth. These vibrations travel through the air as a pressure wave.

A microphone converts these air pressure fluctuations into an electrical voltage. If you plot that voltage on a graph with time on the x-axis, you get a waveform.

Key properties of a sound wave:

  • Frequency: How fast the vibration repeats, measured in Hertz (Hz). A deep bass note might be 80 Hz. A high-pitched whistle might be 8000 Hz.
  • Amplitude: How large the vibration is. Bigger amplitude = louder sound.
  • A real sound is never a single frequency. Speech is a complex mixture of hundreds of different frequencies happening simultaneously.

1.2 What is "Digital" Audio?

To record audio digitally, we use an Analog-to-Digital Converter (ADC). It does two things:

  1. Sampling: It measures the voltage at regular intervals. If we sample at 44,100 times per second, the sampling rate is fs = 44,100 Hz.
  2. Quantization: Each measured voltage is rounded to the nearest number the system can represent.

After this process, your audio is just a long list of numbers (an array) that your MATLAB code manipulates.

x = [0.00, 0.02, 0.05, 0.09, 0.12, 0.14, 0.12, 0.09, ...]

1.3 The Nyquist Theorem

Here is the single most critical concept in all of digital signal processing:

The Nyquist-Shannon Sampling Theorem: To perfectly capture a frequency f in a digital recording, you must sample at at least 2× that frequency. That is, fs ≥ 2f.

The Nyquist frequencyNyquist FrequencyExactly half the sampling rate. If fs = 44,100 Hz, Nyquist is 22,050 Hz. Any frequency above this cannot be recorded properly and will cause distortion called Aliasing. is defined as exactly half the sampling rate.

When we design filters in MATLAB, we don't specify frequencies in raw Hz. Instead, we express them as a normalized frequency: a fraction of the Nyquist limit (between 0.0 and 1.0).

1.4 What is an Equalizer?

An Equalizer (EQ)Audio EqualizerA software or hardware tool that allows you to independently adjust the volume (gain) of specific frequency ranges within an audio signal. decomposes an audio signal into multiple frequency bands, applies a separate gain to each band, and then adds them all back together.

Why not just boost the whole signal?

Because different frequency ranges carry different information:

Frequency RangeWhat it Contains (in Speech)
0–100 HzRumble, room hum, low vibrations. Not much useful speech.
100–300 HzThe "body" or warmth of a voice. The fundamental pitch.
800–2000 HzUpper formants. Critical for speech intelligibility.
2000–5000 Hz"Presence" range. Consonants like "t", "s", "k".

1.5 FIR vs IIR Filters — The Two Families

FIR = Finite Impulse Response

An FIR filter only has feedforward coefficients. It never feeds its own output back into the equation.

  • Always Stable: Since there is no feedback loop, the output can never grow uncontrollably.
  • Linear Phase: This is the most important advantage for audio. All frequencies are delayed by exactly the same amount of time. There is no distortion of the signal's shape.
  • Needs Many Coefficients: To get a sharp frequency cutoff, you need a large number of coefficients (e.g., Order 100).

IIR = Infinite Impulse Response

An IIR filter has both feedforward and feedback coefficients.

  • Very Efficient: A 4th-order IIR filter can achieve a frequency cutoff sharpness that would require an FIR filter with hundreds of coefficients.
  • Non-Linear Phase: Different frequencies get delayed by different amounts. This causes a subtle smearing or pre-ringing effect on speech. Your code fixes this using the filtfilt zero-phase trick!

1.6 Gain in Decibels (dB)

Gain is a logarithmic scale. Why? Because the human ear perceives loudness logarithmically, not linearly.

  • 0 dB: No change.
  • +6 dB: Multiplies amplitude by ~2.0 (noticeably louder).
  • -6 dB: Multiplies amplitude by ~0.5 (noticeably quieter).

1.7 Multirate DSP: Changing the Sample Rate

Your code includes a feature to change the output sample rate.

  • Upsampling by 4×: Inserts zeros, then applies a low-pass filter to interpolate the points. The audio sounds identical but has 4× finer time resolution.
  • Downsampling by 2×: Applies an anti-aliasing low-pass filter FIRST (to remove everything above the new Nyquist limit), then discards every other sample.

1.8 Window Functions: Hamming, Hanning, Blackman

To design an FIR filter, we truncate an infinitely-long ideal mathematical formula. This truncation causes ugly ripples (Gibbs phenomenon). To fix it, we apply a Window FunctionWindow FunctionA smooth mathematical curve that tapers the edges of the filter coefficients to zero, preventing abrupt cutoffs that cause ringing artifacts..

  • Hamming: Great all-around default. Suppresses leaking noise to -53 dB.
  • Blackman: Excellent suppression (-74 dB) but creates a wider (less sharp) transition.
